Turning change into capability

I'm a change management and learning professional who believes the best transformations are built on one thing: understanding people.

I came to this field the long way around. As someone who moved 12 times before turning 21, I learned to make peace with change and even look forward to it. Imagine my surprise when I realized most people typically dread it! That got me thinking: why not make a career out of helping people embrace change?

(Okay, so, I was a teacher first. Because I genuinely love helping people learn. But you get the idea.)

Today, I work at the intersection of organizational change, instructional design, and the very human question of why do people resist things that are good for them? I design strategies and learning experiences that help teams move forward, not because they have to, but because they actually want to.

I'm a problem-solving puzzle lover and a people person. I'll happily spend an hour figuring out why your end users are still emailing PDFs instead of using the system you just spent six months implementing.

If I'm not mapping stakeholder resistance or storyboarding an eLearning module, I'm probably visiting somewhere new — still very much a nomad at heart.

From Inbox to Windchill: Driving Adoption Beyond Go-Live

Enterprise PLM transformation: as it turns out, "we went live" and "people actually use it" are two very different milestones

70%Adoption at Day 1
3xEngagement vs. Baseline
4Comms Phases
5+User Groups Impacted

The Challenge

The client managed product lifecycle communication almost entirely through email, in what they called "lifecycle letters." Implementing PTC Windchill meant dismantling a workflow that users had built their entire professional routines around. The real risk wasn't resistance to the system itself. It was users recreating the email model inside Windchill, essentially treating a structured PLM platform like a very expensive inbox.

Client materials are confidential, visuals not included

My Role

I served as both an OCM Consultant and Instructional Designer, a dual role that gave me visibility from strategy all the way through execution. For this project, I collaborated with client stakeholders, the Windchill solution team, and end users from project initiation through go-live and into post-launch adoption.

OCM Approach: ADKAR Framework

The change strategy was built on Prosci's ADKAR model, ensuring that each phase moved users intentionally forward from knowing the change was coming, all the way through to sustaining new behaviors after launch.

AwarenessDesireKnowledgeAbilityReinforcement

Phase 1: Awareness

Building the Case for Change

By using stakeholder identification, I ensured that all affected parties were aware of the upcoming change. Then I established consistent, early communication which explained the rationale behind the new Windchill implementation and how it would directly address the limitations of the current email-based process. This helped prepare users to begin thinking beyond the existing system and become more receptive to change. In parallel, I also worked to secure leadership alignment on adoption expectations, ensuring that requirements and priorities were clearly defined and consistently reinforced.

Phase 2: Build

Creating Desire and Knowledge

Through change impact analysis across various user groups, I was able to accomplish several key objectives. First, it enabled me to determine which users needed to learn what, allowing me to develop role-based training materials that clearly connected system features to real user workflows. Second, I used this insight to identify and recruit super users, forming a support network that could help guide others and reinforce adoption across teams. Third, by utilizing the super user network, I drove up the interest and excitement for the new implementation.

Phase 3: Go-Live

Enabling Ability

Now it was time to execute the training portion! Through a variety of methods, including ILT sessions, sandbox learning environments, eLearning rollouts, job aids, videos and a go-live readiness checklist, I was able to effectively educate users on how to navigate and utilize Windchill once it was implemented. During this process, I also proactively tried to catch and address resistance in real time by providing targeted support and clarification.

Phase 4: Post Go-Live

Sustaining Reinforcement

Of course, the work doesn't end once implementation is complete! I closely monitored adoption by tracking usage KPIs, click rates, and common user errors to identify gaps in understanding and areas of low engagement. By implementing feedback loops, distributing surveys, and conducting follow-up sessions, I was able to provide support to struggling users and address legacy behaviors as they emerged, which helped to reinforce sustained adoption of the new system.

L&D Approach: ADDIE Methodology

Training was designed using the ADDIE model, grounded in actual user behavior and task analysis, not just system documentation.

Unifying Two Cultures: Ethics and Bias Training for a Merging Organization

Designing a blended learning solution to align values across two companies, because "we're one team now" is a lot easier said than done

2Companies Merged
3Deliverables Built
2Training Modalities
1Unified Program

The Context

LeasePlan was merging with another organization, bringing together two companies with distinct onboarding processes and cultural norms.

My task was to redesign the Ethics training module into a single, unified experience that would work for employees from both organizations.

The challenge wasn't just instructional. It was cultural: the training had to feel like a genuine expression of shared values, not a copy-paste from one company's playbook.

The OCM Angle

Mergers often put employees in a heightened state of uncertainty. They become protective of their work culture and become skeptical towards the other organization and its members. Introducing ethics and bias training in this environment required careful stakeholder communication with leadership to ensure that there was an alignment on the tone, content, and intent of the training before a single slide was even built. The training itself became a signal: what this combined organization stands for and how it treats its people.

What I Built: ADDIE Approach

As a result, my approach to this project was to meet it from several different sides. I created a fully blended learning solution. Starting with an interactive eLearning module for asynchronous delivery and a facilitated ILT session for live cohorts, with two ILT variants developed to deliver the same information but keeping different audience sensitivities in mind.

"See You Next Week": Leading an Overnight Digital Transformation During COVID-19

When the world shut down on a Thursday, a school of 100+ educators needed someone to figure out what "virtual learning" actually meant by Monday

100+Teachers Supported
0Days of Warning
3Platforms Rolled Out
1School Year to Stick the Landing

The Context

On March 11, 2020, I told my students "see you tomorrow" and then never saw them in that classroom again. When Georgia schools shut down overnight due to COVID-19, there was no transition plan, no training, and no roadmap. The district issued a directive to go virtual, with very limited meaningful guidance on how to get there. As the news spread, messages from teachers on the school wide chat started pouring in: How are we going to do this?

My Role

As a 4th grade teacher and grade-level team lead at the time, I became the de facto change leader for my team and eventually the school building. While administration issued top-down mandates, I was on the ground translating those mandates into practical action and supporting teachers who were overwhelmed, undertrained, and in some cases, ready to mail paper worksheets home rather than learn a new system.

The OCM Challenge

This resulted in unplanned, urgent, bottom-up change management: no budget, no formal requirements, no change team, just a brand new reality. Obviously, the resistance was real. Teachers who had taught the same way for decades suddenly faced an entirely new mode of working. The paralysis wasn't caused by laziness, it was due to being overwhelmed. As a younger educator, I had already embraced technology as a normal part of my teaching style. Now, it was my job to make sure that the new tools felt manageable to other educators too, and not just threatening.

The Resistance

Overwhelm Over Opposition

The key thing I realized: Teachers weren't resistant by choice; they were drowning. Many had never used virtual teaching tools or LMS platforms. Given that there was no time to get comfortable and gradually sink into it, many felt that it might have been easier to just send work home rather than face the learning curve.

The Approach

Meet People Where They Are

To help reluctant teachers make the transition as smoothly as possible, I ran weekly professional development sessions, created step-by-step how-to guides, and recorded Screencastify walkthroughs so teachers could learn at their own pace, reducing the fear of asking "basic" questions publicly. I also found other enthusiastic educators who wanted to learn how to utilize additional tools, who then took them back to their own teams.

The Tools

Practical Adoption, Fast

I knew the quickest approach was to guide teachers towards a starting point: eClass, which the school district had already been using for a few years. By using that as a springboard, I gradually added Google Classroom and gSuite for Education into the mix, along with Zoom. After getting their footing back, the county started to provide guides to parents and guardians on how to troubleshoot and tackle IT problems at home. By working together, a whole ecosystem was created.

The Outcome

Daily Adoption by Year End

By the end of the school year, Google Classroom and eClass had become daily tools for teachers across the building, a shift that would have taken years under normal circumstances. In the years following COVID-19, when restrictions were loosened and eventually stopped, the usage of Google Classroom continued to persist. Teachers had fully realized its impact on student learning, as well as its ability to streamline their own workload.

Consulting Methodology

Change Readiness Assessment Framework

How I evaluate whether an organization is truly ready for change, before the change happens

A change readiness assessment shouldn't just be something to check off on a list. It should be used as a diagnostic. Before any strategy is built or training designed, I use a structured assessment across five dimensions to understand where the organization actually stands. For me, these findings shape everything that follows: the OCM approach, the communication plan, the training design, and the go-live timeline. Organizations that skip this step tend to be the ones calling for help six months after launch.

01

Leadership Alignment

I ask myself several questions to get started: Are sponsors actively visible and aligned? Do leaders understand their role in the change, or do they believe "that's what the change team is for"? Misaligned leadership is the single biggest predictor of a failed adoption.

02

Stakeholder Awareness and Desire

When it comes time to raise awareness, I start by understanding whether key stakeholders know why the change is happening, and if they are invested in seeing the change succeed. My reasoning is that awareness without desire creates only compliance. Meanwhile, a desire without awareness creates chaos. Both a desire for the change and an awareness of why the change must be done are needed for successful adoption.

03

Organizational Capacity for Change

Another thing to consider that might get overlooked is how much change is already occurring in the organization. Overwhelming individuals with too many changes all at once leads to change saturation, and a failed adoption of an otherwise good change. I assess where the current change load is before recommending pace and sequencing for any upcoming changes in the pipeline.

04

Training and Knowledge Readiness

One mistake I see often in organizations is when they are unsure of what exactly people need to know, and when they need to know it. To combat this, I map knowledge gaps in specific user groups and then work from there to build a training timeline that doesn't dump everything onto users in week one. Additionally, it is just as important to realize that not all users need to learn the same things. Role-specific changes can help mitigate becoming overwhelmed.

05

Resistance Risk Profile

I also consider where resistance is most likely to emerge, and why. This is because resistance is almost always rational from the person feeling it: they have a good reason for sticking to legacy processes and ideas. By identifying it early and understanding its root cause, I have gone from being a reactive change management consultant to a proactive one.

+

What Comes Next

Finally, I cannot stress enough how useful assessments can be. Their findings feed directly into all aspects of OCM strategy, including the stakeholder engagement plan, communication approach, and training design. Use assessments to your advantage! Create deliverables that are connected to what the assessments revealed, whether pre-, mid- or post-implementation. This ensures that your change strategy remains targeted, relevant, and effective throughout its entire lifecycle.
